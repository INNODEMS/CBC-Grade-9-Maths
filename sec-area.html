    Section 3.1 Area

    In our day-to-day life, we often encounter shapes and objects whether it’s the surface of a table, the wall of a house, the face of a pyramid, or even a football. Understanding how to calculate areas and surface areas helps us solve real-life problems, such as finding how much paint is needed, how much material is required to build something, or how much space an object occupies. This topic will help you explore how to calculate area and surface area of various 2D and 3D shapes in real and practical situations.
    🔗
    \(\textbf{Learning Expectations}\)
    🔗
    By the end of this section, you should be able to:
    🔗
    • Calculate the area of pentagons and hexagons in various situations.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Find the surface area of triangular-based and rectangular-based prisms.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Work out the surface area of pyramids with triangular, rectangular, and square bases.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Calculate the area of a sector and segment of a circle.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Find the surface area of a cone and relate it to real-life situations.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Calculate the surface area of a sphere in practical contexts.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    You will also build skills in problem-solving, logical reasoning, and using mathematics in real life. Let’s begin exploring the world of shapes and spaces!
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.1 Area of regular and irregular polygons

    \(\textbf{Polygon}\)
    🔗
    A polygon is a closed 2D shape made up of straight lines. Examples include \(\textbf{triangles, quadrilaterals, pentagons}\) and \(\textbf{hexagons.}\)
    🔗
    \(\textbf{Regular Polygons}\)
    🔗
    A polygon is regular if:
    🔗
    • All sides are equal in length.
      🔗
      🔗
    • All interior angles are equal. eg equilateral triangle, square, regular pentagon, regular hexagon
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    \(\textbf{Irregular Polygons}\)
    🔗
    A polygon is irregular if:
    🔗
    • Sides are not all the same length, and/or
      🔗
      🔗
    • Angles are not all equal.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    🔗
    \(\text{Area}=\frac{1}{2} \times \text{Perimeter(P)} \times \text{apothem(a)}\)
    🔗
    Where \(\text{Perimeter} = \text{Length} \times 6\) and \(\textbf{Apothem}\) is the distance from the center of a regular hexagon to a side.

    🔗

    🔗

    🔗
    Therefore:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \text{Area} =\amp \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2} \times \text{s}^2\\ =\amp \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2} \times (7.5)^2\\ =\amp \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2} \times 56.25\\ =\amp 84.375\sqrt{3} \, \text{km}^2\\ ≈ \amp 146.41\, \text{km}^2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    This implies that, the area of one hexagonal cell is approximately \({\color{blue}146.41 \, \text{km}^2}\text{.}\)

    🔗
    \begin{align*} \text{Area} =\amp \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2} \times \text{s}^2 \end{align*}

    Therefore, the side length of the regular hexagon is \({\color{blue}6 \, \text{cm}}\text{.}\)

    Subsection 3.1.2 Surface area of prism

    A \(\textbf{prism}\) A prism is a solid with uniform cross-section.
    🔗
    \(\textbf{Features of a prism:}\)
    🔗
    1. The two bases are exactly the same in shape and size.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The side faces are parallelograms (usually rectangles).
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The name of a prism comes from the shape of its base.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    \(\textbf{For Example:}\)
    🔗
    1. A prism with a triangular base is called a \(\textbf{triangular prism}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. A prism with a rectangular base is called a \(\textbf{rectangular prism}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    The surface area of a prism is the total area of all its faces that means the areas of the two identical bases and all the side faces (called lateral faces).
    🔗

      🔗
      \(\text{Surface Area} = 2 \times \text{Area of triangular base} + \text{Area of rectangular face 1} + \text{Area of rectangular face 2} + \text{Area of rectangular face 3}\)
      🔗

    Example 3.1.8.
    A triangular prism has a base of \(5 \, \text{cm}\text{,}\) height \(4\,\text{cm}\text{,}\) and prism height \(8 \, \text{cm}\text{.}\) The other two sides of the triangle are \(5 \, \text{cm}\) and \(6 \, \text{cm}\text{.}\) Find its surface area.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Area of the triangular base:
    🔗
    \(\text{Area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 5 \, \text{cm} \times 4 \, \text{cm} = 10 \, \text{cm}^2\)
    🔗
    Area of the rectangular faces:
    🔗
    \(\text{Area of rectangular face 1} = \text{base} \times \text{height of prism} = 5 \, \text{cm} \times 8 \, \text{cm} = 40 \, \text{cm}^2\)
    🔗
    Now area of the triangular faces is given by:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} Area= \amp 2 \times \frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 4 \\ = \amp 20 \, \text{cm}^2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Area of the rectangular faces is:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} Area= \amp 5 \times 8 + 6 \times 8 + 5 \times 8\\ = \amp 40 + 48 + 40 = 128 \, \text{cm}^2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the total surface area of the prism is:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} Surface Area = \amp 2 \times 10 + 40 + 48 + 40\\ = \amp 20 + 128 = 148 \, \text{cm}^2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    \({\color{blue}\text{Total Surface Area} = 148 \, \text{cm}^2}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.4 Area of a part of a circle

    Definition 3.1.30.

    A \(\textbf{sphere}\) is a solid that is entirely round with every point on the surface at anequal distance from the centre.
    🔗

    The distance from the center to any point on the surface is called the \(\textbf{radius}\text{.}\)

    Example 3.1.34.

    The balls used in a golf tournament have diameter of \(4.2 \text{cm}\) and \(4.4 \,\text{cm}\text{.}\) What is the difference in the surface area between the largest and smallest golf ball?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The diameter of the smallest ball is \(4.2\,\text{cm}\text{,}\) so its radius is \(r_1 = \frac{4.2}{2} = 2.1\,\text{cm}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The diameter of the largest ball is \(4.4\,\text{cm}\text{,}\) so its radius is \(r_2 = \frac{4.4}{2} = 2.2\,\text{cm}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The formula for the surface area of a sphere is:
    \begin{equation*} \text{SA} = 4\pi r^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For the smallest ball:
    \begin{align*} \text{SA}_1 = \amp 4 \times \pi \times (2.1)^2\\ = \amp 4 \times \pi \times 4.41\\ = \amp 17.64\pi \end{align*}
    🔗
    For the largest ball:
    \begin{align*} \text{SA}_2 = \amp 4 \times \pi \times (2.2)^2\\ = \amp 4 \times \pi \times 4.84\\ = \amp 19.36\pi \end{align*}
    🔗
    The difference in surface area is:
    \begin{align*} \text{Difference} = \amp 19.36\pi - 17.64\pi\\ = \amp 1.72\pi \end{align*}
    If \(\pi = 3.142\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \text{Difference} = \amp 1.72 \times 3.142\\ = \amp 5.40224\,\text{cm}^2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the difference in surface area between the largest and smallest golf ball is \({\color{blue}5.40\,\text{cm}^2}\) .
