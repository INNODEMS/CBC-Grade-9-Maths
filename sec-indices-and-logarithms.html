    Section 1.3 Indices and Logarithms

    Indices and logarithms are mathematical concepts that deal with powers and roots of numbers. They are used to simplify calculations involving large numbers and to express relationships between quantities in a more manageable form.
    🔗
    Indices, also known as exponents, represent the number of times a base is multiplied by itself. For example, in the expression \(a^n\text{,}\) \(a\) is the base and \(n\) is the exponent or index. This means that \(a\) is multiplied by itself \(n\) times.
    🔗
    Logarithms, on the other hand, are the inverse operation of exponentiation. The logarithm of a number is the exponent to which a base must be raised to produce that number. For example, if \(b^x = y\text{,}\) then \(x = \log_b(y)\text{,}\) where \(b\) is the base and \(y\) is the number.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.3.1 Expressing numbers in index form

    Index form means a number written with a base and an exponent (also called index or power).
    🔗
    \(\textbf{Base}\) is the number that is multiplied by itself, and \(\textbf{exponent}\) (or index) tells how many times the base is used in the multiplication.
    🔗
    A number in index form is written as \(a^n\text{,}\) where \(a\) is the base and \(n\) is the exponent.
    🔗
    \(\textbf{To express a number in index form, you can follow these steps:}\)
    🔗
    1. Identify the base of the number you want to express in index form.
    🔗
    2. Determine the exponent or index, which is the number of times the base is multiplied by itself to obtain the original number.
    🔗
    3. Write the number in the form \(a^n\text{,}\) where \(a\) is the base and \(n\) is the exponent.
    🔗
    For example, the number \(8\) can be expressed in index form as \(2^3\text{,}\) since \(2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Expressing numbers in index form is useful for simplifying calculations, especially when dealing with large numbers or when performing operations like multiplication and division.

    \({\color{blue}2}\) \(32\)

    Subsection 1.3.2 Laws of indices

    The laws of indices, also known as the laws of exponents, are fundamental rules that govern the manipulation of expressions involving powers or indices. These laws are essential for simplifying calculations and solving equations involving exponential terms. Here are the key laws of indices:
    🔗
    1. \(\textbf{Product of Powers}\text{:}\) When multiplying two expressions with the same base, you add the exponents: \(a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    2. \(\textbf{Quotient of Powers}\text{:}\) When dividing two expressions with the same base, you subtract the exponents: \(\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}\text{,}\) where \(a \neq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    3. \(\textbf{Power of a Power}\text{:}\) When raising an expression with an exponent to another exponent, you multiply the exponents: \((a^m)^n = a^{m \times n}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    4. \(\textbf{Power of a Product}\text{:}\) When raising a product to an exponent, you raise each factor to the exponent: \((ab)^n = a^n \times b^n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    5. \(\textbf{Power of a Quotient}\text{:}\) When raising a quotient to an exponent, you raise both the numerator and denominator to the exponent: \(\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}\text{,}\) where \(b \neq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    6. \(\textbf{Zero Exponent}\text{:}\) Any non-zero base raised to the power of zero is equal to one: \(a^0 = 1\text{,}\) where \(a \neq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    7. \(\textbf{Negative Exponent}\text{:}\) A negative exponent indicates the reciprocal of the base raised to the absolute value of the exponent: \(a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}\text{,}\) where \(a \neq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Subsubsection 1.3.2.1 Multiplication law of indices

    Subsection 1.3.3 Zero indices

    For any natural number \(a\text{,}\) if it is raised to the power of zero, the result is always \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This means that \(a^0 = 1\) for any \(a \neq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \(\frac{a}{a} = 1\text{,}\) but from indices, \(\frac{a}{a} = a^0\) , \(a^0 = 1\) for any natural number \(a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Example:
    🔗
    Lets take \(a\) to be \(4\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \(4^2 \div 4^2 = \frac{4^2}{4^2} = 1\)
    🔗
    But using the laws of indices:
    🔗
    \(4^2 \div 4^2 = 4^{2-2} = 4^0\)
    🔗
    So: \(4^0 = 1\)
    🔗
    Suppose we have \(\frac{5^3}{5^3}\)
    🔗
    \(\frac{5^3}{5^3} = 1\)
    🔗
    But using the laws of indices:
    🔗
    \(\frac{5^3}{5^3} = 5^{3-3} = 5^0\)
    🔗
    Thus: \(5^0 = 1\)
    🔗
    Also, if we have \(\frac{8}{8}\) the result is \(1\text{.}\) This can be done using powers of numbers:
    🔗
    That is, \(\frac{8}{8} = 2^3 \div 2^3 = 2^{3-3}= 2^0 = 1\)
    🔗
    Therefore, any natural number raised to the power of zero is equal to \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We can also use pattern recognition to verify that \(a^0 = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Example: Powers of \(2\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \(2^3=8\)
    🔗
    \(2^2=4\)
    🔗
    \(2^1=2\)
    🔗
    \(2^0=1\)
    🔗
    Each step divides by \(2\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \(8 \div 2=4, 4 \div 2= 2 , 2 \div2=1\)
    🔗
    Thus \(2^0 = 1\)
    🔗

    Subsection 1.3.4 Negative indices

    Negative indices are used to represent the reciprocal of a number raised to a positive exponent. When a base is raised to a negative exponent, it indicates that the base should be taken as the reciprocal and then raised to the absolute value of the exponent.
    🔗
    For example, if we have a base \(a\) and a negative exponent \(-n\text{,}\) it can be expressed as: \(a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}\text{.}\) This means that instead of multiplying \(a\) by itself \(n\) times, we take the reciprocal of \(a^n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Negative indices are particularly useful in simplifying expressions and solving equations involving powers. They allow us to express division in terms of multiplication, making calculations more straightforward.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.3.5 Fractional indices

    Fractional indices, also known as rational indices, are used to represent roots of numbers in index form. A fractional index indicates that the base is raised to a power and then the root is taken.
    🔗
    For example, if we have a base \(a\) and a fractional index \(\frac{m}{n}\text{,}\) it can be expressed as: \(a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a^m}\text{.}\) This means that we first raise \(a\) to the power of \(m\) and then take the \(n\)-th root of the result.

    Example 1.3.13.

    Find the value of \(x\) in:
    🔗
    1. \(\displaystyle 2^{2x}=16\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle 3x^2 = 18\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle 3^{2x-4} = 1\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(\displaystyle 4^{-x} = \frac{1}{16}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. To solve for \(2^{2x}=16\text{:}\)
      🔗
      First express \(16\) as a prime factor in index form
      🔗
      \(16 = 2^4\)
      🔗
      Hence, \(2^{2x} = 2^4\)
      🔗
      Next we equate the powers:
      🔗
      \(2x=4\)
      🔗
      Hence, \(x = 2\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(3x^2 = 18\)
      🔗
      Here, we divide both sides by the coefficient of \(x\) which is \(3\)
      🔗
      \(\frac{3x^2}{3} = \frac{18}{3}\)
      🔗
      \(x^2 = 6\)
      🔗
      Applying laws of indices we have:
      🔗
      \((x^2)^{1/2}= 6^{1/2}\)
      🔗
      Hence, \(x= 6^{1/2}\)
      🔗
      \(= \sqrt{6}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(3^{2x-4} = 1\)
      🔗
      According to definition of zero indices, any number \(a^0 = 1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      Hence,
      🔗
      \(3^{2x-4} = 3^0\)
      🔗
      Since the bases are the same, we equate the powers:
      🔗
      \(2x-4 = 0\)
      🔗
      \(2x=4\)
      🔗
      \(x=2\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(4^{-x} = \frac{1}{16}\)
      🔗
      To solve for \(x\text{,}\) we can write \(\frac{1}{16}\) as \(16^{-1}\)
      🔗
      So, \(4^{-x} = 16^{-1}\)
      🔗
      Next, we need to express \(16\) to be of base \(4\) so that both sides have equal bases.
      🔗
      Since, \(16 = 4^2\) then \(16^{-1} =(4^2)^{-1}\)
      🔗
      \((4^2)^{-1}= 4^{2 \times -1} = 4^{-2}\)
      🔗
      Now, \(4^{-x}=4^{-2}\)
      🔗
      Equating the indices: \(-x=-2\)
      🔗
      \(x = 2\)
      🔗
      🔗

    Subsection 1.3.7 Powers of 10 and common logarithms

    🔗
    \(\bullet\) \(\textbf{Powers of 10 }\) are numbers that represent a base of \(10\) raised to an exponent.
    🔗
    The common logarithm, denoted as \(\log_{10}\text{,}\) is the logarithm to the base \(10\text{.}\) It is used to express numbers in terms of powers of \(10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The common logarithm of a number \(x\) is defined as the exponent to which the base \(10\) must be raised to obtain \(x\text{.}\) In mathematical terms, if \(y = \log_{10}(x)\text{,}\) then \(10^y = x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For example, if \(x = 1000\text{,}\) then \(\log_{10}(1000) = 3\) because \(10^3 = 1000\text{.}\) This means that \(1000\) can be expressed as \(10^3\text{,}\) which is a power of \(10\text{.}\)
    🔗
