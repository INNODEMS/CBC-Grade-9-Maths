    Section 1.4 Compound proportions and Rates of work

    \(\textbf{Compound Proportions}\)
    🔗
    Consider the numbers \(4, 10, 20\) and \(50\text{.}\) \(4:10 = 2:5\) and \(20:50 = 2:5 \text{.}\) The two ratios are equal \((4:10 = 20:50)\text{.}\) Therefore, these \(4\) numbers are said to be proportional.
    🔗
    Generally, any \(4\) numbers \(a, b, c\) and \(d\) are said to be proportional if:
    🔗
    \(\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}\)
    🔗
    or
    🔗
    \(\frac{a}{c} = \frac{b}{d} \)
    🔗
    \(\textbf{Rate of work}\) is the amount of work done per unit of time, often expressed as work divided by time
    🔗
    \(\textbf{Uses of proportions in real life}\)
    🔗
    Proportions are everywhere in our daily life, and are very useful. For example, when preparing a meal for many people you would need to increase the amount of ingredients. To do this without altering the taste, you would have to maintain the proportions between various ingredients. They are also important for determining relationships. If you were at a shop and told an item has a discount of sh \(50\) but you do not know its original price, this would not be useful information. However, if you are instead told it is \(15%\) off, it has a larger effect as you now know the proportion of the new price versus the old price.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.4.1 Proportional Parts

    \(\textbf{Proportion}\) is an equation in which two ratios are set equal to each other. For example, if \(a\) and \(b\) are in the same ratio as \(c\) and \(d\text{,}\) we can write this as:
    🔗
    \(a:b = c:d\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This means that the fraction \(\frac{a}{b}\) is equal to the fraction \(\frac{c}{d}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Generally, if an amount \(m\) is to be divided in the ratio \(a:b:c\text{,}\) then the parts will be divided as follows:
    🔗
    1. \(a\) will get \(\frac{a}{a+b+c} \times m\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(b\) will get \(\frac{b}{a+b+c} \times m\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(c\) will get \(\frac{c}{a+b+c} \times m\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.4.1.

    A basket of fruits contains mangoes, oranges, and bananas in the ratio of \(3:2:5\text{.}\) If the basket contains \(100\) fruits in total, how many of each type of fruit are there?
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find the number of each type of fruit, we first calculate the total parts in the ratio: \(3 + 2 + 5 = 10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hence number of proportional parts \(= 10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \text{Number of mangoes} =\amp \frac{3}{10} \times \text{number of fruits}. \\ =\amp \frac{3}{10} \times 100 \\ =\amp 30 \\ \text{Number of oranges} =\amp \frac{2}{10} \times \text{number of fruits}. \\ =\amp \frac{2}{10} \times 100 \\ =\amp 20 \\ \text{Number of bananas} =\amp \frac{5}{10} \times \text{number of fruits}. \\ =\amp \frac{5}{10} \times 100 \\ =\amp 50 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.4.2.

    What is the value of \(z\) in \(12 : z = 4 : 5?\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since \(a:b = c:d\) can be written as \(\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}\text{,}\) then to find the value of \(z\text{,}\) we can set up the proportion:
    🔗
    \(\frac{12}{z} = \frac{4}{5}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Cross-multiplying gives us: \(12 \times 5 = 4 \times z\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Simplifying this, we have: \(60 = 4z\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Dividing both sides by \(4\) gives us: \(z = \frac{60}{4} = 15\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, the value of \(z\) is \(15\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.4.2 Relating different ratios in real life

    In real life, we often encounter situations where we need to relate different ratios or rates of work. For example, if James a Grade \(9\) student can complete a math problem in a \(5\) minutes, and Esther also a Grade \(10\) learner completes the same task in \(4\) minutes, we can express their work rates as ratios. By understanding these ratios, we can determine how long it will take for them to complete the task together or how their individual contributions compare.
    🔗
    Consider the numbers \(3, 6, 12\) and \(24\text{.}\) You may notice that each number is equal to the previous number multiplied by \(2\text{.}\) The \(4\) numbers are therefore said to be in continued proportion.
    🔗
    Generally, if \(4\) numbers, \(a, b, c\) and \(d\) are in continued proportion:
    🔗
    \(\frac{a}{b} = \frac{b}{c} = \frac{c}{d}\)
    🔗
    Since \(\frac{a}{b} = \frac{b}{c},\) then \(b^2 = ac\)
    🔗
    \(b = \pm \sqrt{ac} \)
    🔗
    If numbers are in continued proportion, they form a geometric progression since they have a common ratio.
    🔗

    Example 1.4.3.

    If the ratio of the ages of John and Mary is \(3:4\text{,}\) and the ratio of their ages in \(5\) years will be \(4:5\text{,}\) what are their current ages?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let John’s current age be \(3x\) and Mary’s current age be \(4x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In \(5\) years, John’s age will be \(3x + 5\) and Mary’s age will be \(4x + 5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    According to the problem, we have:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} \frac{3x + 5}{4x + 5} = \frac{4}{5} \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Cross-multiplying gives us:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} 5(3x + 5) = 4(4x + 5) \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Simplifying this, we have:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} 15x + 25 = 16x + 20 \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Rearranging gives us:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} x = 5 \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Therefore, John’s current age is:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} 3 \times 5 = 15\text{,} \end{gather*}
    and Mary’s current age is:
    \begin{gather*} 4 \times 5 = 20\text{.} \end{gather*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.4.4.

    Given that the ratio \(x:y=2:3\text{,}\) find the ratio \((5x−2y):(x+y)\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We start with the given ratio \(x:y=2:3\text{.}\) This means we can express \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of a common variable, say \(k\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} x = 2k\\ y = 3k \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Now, we need to find the ratio \((5x−2y):(x+y)\text{.}\) Substituting the values of \(x\) and \(y\) gives us:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} 5x - 2y = 5(2k) - 2(3k) = 10k - 6k = 4k\\ x + y = 2k + 3k = 5k \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the ratio becomes:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} (5x−2y):(x+y) = 4k:5k \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Simplifying this ratio, we can cancel out \(k\) (assuming \(k ≠ 0\)):
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} 4:5 \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the ratio \((5x−2y):(x+y)\) simplifies to \(4:5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.4.5.

    If \(22, X\) and \(88\) are in continued proportion, find the value of \(X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    If \(22, X\) and \(88\) are in continued proportion, then we can express this relationship as:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} \frac{22}{X} = \frac{X}{88} \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Cross-multiplying gives us:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} 22 \times 88 = X^2 \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Simplifying this, we have:
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} 1936 = X^2 \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Hence \(X = \sqrt{1936}\)
    🔗
    \(= 44 \)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.4.3 Working out compound proportions using the ratio method

    Two quantities in compound proportion increases or decreases in the same ratio.
    🔗
    To work out compound proportions using the ratio method, you can follow these steps:
    🔗
    1. Identify the two ratios you want to compare or relate.
    🔗
    2. Set up a proportion by equating the two ratios.
    🔗
    3. Cross-multiply to eliminate the fractions.
    🔗
    4. Solve for the unknown variable, if applicable.
    🔗
    5. Check your solution by substituting back into the original ratios to ensure they are equal.
    🔗
    For example, if you have the ratios \(3:4\) and \(6:8\text{,}\) you can set up the proportion as follows:
    🔗
    \(\frac{3}{4} = \frac{6}{8}\)
    🔗
    Cross-multiplying gives you \(3 \times 8 = 4 \times 6\text{,}\) which simplifies to \(24\text{.}\) This confirms that the two ratios are equivalent.
    🔗
    🔗

    A class has \(8\) girls and \(6\) boys. More students are admitted to the class such that the ratio of girls to boys remain th same.
    🔗
    The new number of boys in the class is now \(12\text{.}\) Determine the new number of girls in the class using the ratio method.

    🔗

    🔗
    \(y: 12 = 4:3\)
    🔗

    🔗
    Now, cross-multiplying to solve for \(y\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \(3y = 4 \times 12\)
    🔗
    \(3y = 48\)
    🔗
    \(y = \frac{48}{3} = 16\)
    🔗
    So, the new number of girls in the class is \(16\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Subsection 1.4.4 Rates of work

    The rate of work is a measure of how quickly work is done or how much work is completed in a given amount of time. It is often expressed as the amount of work done per unit of time, such as hours, days, or weeks.
    🔗
    When dealing with rates of work, the key is to find out how much work a person/thing can do in one hour, one day, or any other time unit.

    Example 1.4.8.

    A group of workers can complete a task in \(8\) hours. If they work at the same rate, how long will it take them to complete the same task if they double their effort?
    🔗
    Solution.
    If the group of workers can complete a task in \(8\) hours, doubling their effort means they will work twice as fast.
    🔗
    Therefore, the time taken to complete the same task when they double their effort will be half of \(8\) hours.
    🔗
    \(\text{Time taken} = \frac{8}{2} = 4 \text{ hours}\)
    🔗
    So, it will take them \(4\) hours to complete the same task if they double their effort.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.4.9.

    A factory produces \(100\) units of a product in \(5\) hours. If the factory increases its production rate by \(20%\text{,}\) how many units will it produce in \(5\) hours?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The factory’s current production rate is \(100\) units in \(5\) hours, which means it produces \(20\) units per hour.
    🔗
    If the factory increases its production rate by \(20%\text{,}\) the new production rate will be:
    🔗
    \(\text{New production rate} = 20 + (20 \times 0.20) = 20 + 4 = 24 \text{ units per hour}\)
    🔗
    Now, to find out how many units the factory will produce in \(5\) hours at the new production rate:
    🔗
    \(\text{Units produced in 5 hours} = 24 \times 5 = 120 \text{ units}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, the factory will produce \(120\) units in \(5\) hours after increasing its production rate by \(20%\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 1.4.10.

    Mary, John and Essy did a piece of work in \(45\) hours, \(40\) hours and \(30\) hours respectively. How long can John take to complete the work when he starts after Mary and Essy have worked for \(13\) hours each?
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we need to find out the amount of work done by Mary, John, and Essy in one hour.
    🔗
    The work done by each person in one hour is:
    🔗
    \(\text{Mary's rate} = \frac{1}{45} \text{ of the work per hour}\)
    🔗
    \(\text{John's rate} = \frac{1}{40} \text{ of the work per hour}\)
    🔗
    \(\text{Essy's rate} = \frac{1}{30} \text{ of the work per hour}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, the amount of work Mary and Essy will do in \(1\) hour is:
    🔗
    \(\frac{1}{45} + \frac{1}{30} = \frac{2}{90} + \frac{3}{90} = \frac{5}{90} \)
    🔗
    \(= \frac{1}{18} \text{ of the work per hour}\)
    🔗
    For \(13\) hours, Mary and Essy will have done:
    🔗
    \(13 \times \frac{1}{18} = \frac{13}{18} \text{ of the work}\)
    🔗
    The remaining work after Mary and Essy have worked for \(13\) hours is:
    🔗
    \(1 - \frac{13}{18} = \frac{5}{18} \text{ of the work}\)
    🔗
    Hence, to find out how long it will take John to complete the remaining work, we can use his rate of work:
    🔗
    \(\text{Time taken by John} = \frac{\text{Remaining work}}{\text{John's rate}} = \frac{\frac{5}{18}}{\frac{1}{40}} = \frac{5}{18} \times 40 \)
    🔗
    \(= \frac{200}{18} = \frac{100}{9}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, John will take approximately \(11\frac{1}{9}\) hours to complete the remaining work after Mary and Essy have worked for \(13\) hours each.
