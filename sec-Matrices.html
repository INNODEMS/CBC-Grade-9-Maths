    Section 2.1 Matrices

    What is a Matrix?

    A matrix (plural: matrices) is a rectangular arrangement of numbers, symbols, or expressions that are organized in rows and columns, and enclosed by brackets.
    Each number in a matrix is called an \(\textbf{entry}\) or \(\textbf{element}\text{.}\) The position of each element is identified by its row number and column number.
    \(\textbf{Basic Structure Example:}\)
    Let matrix \(A = \displaystyle \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 \end {pmatrix}\)
    This matrix has 2 rows and 2 columns, so we call it a \(\textbf{2 times 2}\) matrix (read: "2 by 2 matrix").
    The number \(2\) is in the \(\textbf{first row, first column}\text{.}\)
    The number \(5\) is in the \(\textbf{second row, second column}\text{.}\)

    Subsection 2.1.1 Identifying a Matrix in Different Situations

    A \(\textbf{matrix}\) is a way of organizing numbers or information in rows and columnsjust like the tables you have seen in other topics such as measurement and data handling. Matrices help us organize, compare, and analyze data efficiently.
    \(\textbf{Did you know?}\) The tables you used for currency exchange, frequency distributions, or measurement results are all examples of data that can be represented as matrices!
    Matrices are powerful tools for organizing and analyzing real-world data, such as sports scores, business sales, or scientific results. By turning tables into matrices, we can use mathematical methods to solve problems more easily.

    Activity 2.1.1.

    The Greenfield School Cafeteria prepares meals for students in three school levels: \(\textbf{Lower Primary}\text{,}\) \(\textbf{Upper Primary}\text{,}\) and \(\textbf{Secondary}\text{.}\) Each week, they prepare two types of meals: \(\textbf{Vegetarian}\) and \(\textbf{Non-Vegetarian}\text{.}\) The number of meals prepared (in hundreds) for Week 1 and Week 2 is recorded in the tables below:
    \(\textbf{Week 1 Meal Data}\)
    4 6
    3 5
    2 7
    \(\textbf{Week 2 Meal Data}\)
    5 4
    4 6
    3 8
    Use the data in the tables to answer the following questions:
    1. How many rows are in each table? What does each row represent?
    2. How many columns are in each table? What does each column represent?
    3. Write the Week 1 data as a matrix using the square brackets \(\begin{bmatrix} .. \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    4. Write the Week 2 data as a matrix using the curly brackets \(\begin{pmatrix} .. \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\)
    5. Compare the matrices from Week 1 and Week 2. In which categories did the cafeteria increase or reduce production?
    Key Take Away
    A matrix is a rectangular arrangement of numbers or data in rows and columns. It can represent real-life situations, such as meal preparation data, sports scores, or sales figures.

    Example 2.1.1.

    The following table shows the number of books read by three students (Alice, Brian, and Carol) in two terms:
    Term 1 Term 2
    Alice 4 5
    Brian 3 6
    Carol 5 4
    Write this table as a matrix, where each row is a student and each column is a term. Then answer: Who read the most books in Term 2? How many books did Brian read in total?
    Solution.
    Step 1: Write the matrix:
    \(\textbf{Matrix:}\) \(\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \\ 5 & 4 \\ \end{bmatrix}\)
    Rows: Alice, Brian, Carol.
    Columns: Term 1, Term 2.
    • Who read the most books in Term 2? \(\textbf{Brian}\)
    • We compare the Term2 column: Alice = 5, Brian = 6, Carol = 4. The highest number is \(\textbf{6}\text{,}\) which belongs to \(\textbf{Brian}\text{.}\)
    • How many books did Brian read in total?
    • We add the numbers in Brians row: 3 (Term 1) + 6 (Term 2) = 9. therefore he read a total of \(\textbf{9 books}\text{.}\)

    Subsection 2.1.2 Determining the Order of a Matrix in Different Situations

    The order of a matrix tells us how many rows and columns it has. This is written as rows columns (for example, 2 3 means 2 rows and 3 columns). Knowing the order helps us describe, compare, and use tables and schedules correctly in real life.
    \(Did you know?\) The order of a matrix is like the size of a tableif you know the number of rows and columns, you know how much information it can hold!

    Activity 2.1.2.

    The following table shows the number of students in four different classes (A, B, C, D) over three days:
    Day 1 Day 2 Day 3
    Class A 30 28 29
    Class B 27 29 28
    Class C 25 26 27
    Class D 32 31 30
    Use the data in the table to answer the following questions:
    1. Write the table as a matrix \(S\text{.}\)
    2. What is the order of matrix \(S\text{?}\)
    3. What does each row and each column represent?
    4. If you wanted to add another class, how would the order of the matrix change?
    Key Take Away
    The order of a matrix helps us describe and use tables, schedules, and data sets correctly in real life. It tells us how much information the matrix can hold.

    Example 2.1.2.

    The following matrix shows the number of books borrowed by three students (Alice, Brian, Carol) in four months:
    \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 \\ 3 & 6 & 5 & 4 \\ 5 & 4 & 3 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\)
    What is the order of matrix \(B\text{?}\) What does the entry in the second row, third column represent?
    Solution.
    Step 1: Count the number of rows.
    There are 3 rows (each row represents a student).
    Step 2: Count the number of columns.
    There are 4 columns (each column represents a month).
    Step 3: Write the order as rows columns.
    So, the order is 3 4.
    Step 4: Interpret the meaning.
    The entry in the second row, third column (5) means Brian borrowed 5 books in Month 3.

    Subsection 2.1.3 Determining the Position of Items in a Matrix in Different Situations

    Every number or item in a matrix has a special place, just like a seat in a classroom or a product on a shelf. We use the row and column numbers to find the exact position of each item. This helps us quickly locate information in tables, scoreboards, or lists.
    \(Did you know?\) The position of an item in a matrix is written as (row, column). For example, (2,3) means row 2, column 3.

    Activity 2.1.3.

    \(\textbf{Map Matrix Adventure}\)
    Imagine a 33 grid representing a simple map. Each cell can have a value: 0 (empty), 1 (tree), 2 (house), 3 (treasure). The map is represented by the following matrix:
    \(M = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}\)
    1. What is at position (2,2)? What does it represent?
    2. List all the positions where there is a house.
    3. If you start at (1,1) and move to (2,2), what do you find?
    4. Draw a simple sketch of the map using the matrix values as symbols.
    Key Take Away
    The position (row, column) helps us locate and understand data in real-life matrices, maps, and grids. It makes finding information quick and fun!

    Example 2.1.3.

    Lets solve the map matrix puzzle:
    \(M = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}\)
    1. \(\textbf{What is at position (2,2)?}\) The value is 3, which represents the treasure.
    2. \(\textbf{List all the positions where there is a house (2):}\)
    • (2,1)
    • (3,2)
    3. \(\textbf{If you start at (1,1) and move to (2,2), what do you find?}\) You find the treasure!
    4. \(\textbf{Draw a simple sketch of the map:}\) (Students draw a 33 grid and use symbols for each value: 0=empty, 1=tree, 2=house, 3=treasure.)
    Solution.
    Step 1: Look up the value at (2,2):
    The value is 3 (\(\textbf{treasure}\)).
    Step 2: Find all positions with value 2 (house):
    • (2,1)
    • (3,2)
    Step 3: Moving from (1,1) to (2,2) leads to the treasure.
    Step 4: Students draw a grid and fill in the symbols for each value.

    Subsection 2.1.4 Determining Compatibility of Matrices in Addition and Subtraction

    In real life, we often want to combine or compare data from different sourceslike adding up sales from two weeks, or comparing scores from two rounds of a game. To do this with matrices, the data must be organized in the same way. This means the matrices must have the same number of rows and columns (the same \(order\)).
    \(Did you know?\) You can only add or subtract matrices if they are the same size! This is just like adding two tables with the same number of rows and columns.
    Lets see how this works with a real-life example.

    Activity 2.1.4.

    The table below shows the number of books borrowed from a library by three students (Alice, Brian, and Carol) in two different months:
    Month 1 Month 2
    Alice 4 5
    Brian 3 6
    Carol 5 4
    The next month, the same students borrowed the following number of books:
    Month 3 Month 4
    Alice 6 3
    Brian 2 7
    Carol 4 5
    Use the data in the tables to answer the following questions:
    1. Write the Month 1 & 2 data as a matrix \(A\) and the Month 3 & 4 data as a matrix \(B\text{.}\)
    2. Can you add matrices \(A\) and \(B\text{?}\) Why or why not?
    3. Suppose you had a table for a different group of students with only two rows. Could you add this to matrix \(A\text{?}\)
    4. What does the sum \(A + B\) represent in this context?
    Key Take Away
    You can only add or subtract matrices if they have the same order (same number of rows and columns). This allows us to combine or compare real-life data correctly, just like adding up tables with the same structure.

    Example 2.1.4.

    The following matrices show the number of items sold by two shops over two days:
    \(Shop\ 1: \quad A = \begin{bmatrix} 10 & 12 \\ 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix}\) \(Shop\ 2: \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 5 \\ 6 & 11 \end{bmatrix}\)
    Can you add these matrices? If so, what does the result mean?
    Solution.
    Step 1: Check the order of both matrices.
    Matrix \(A\text{:}\) 2 rows, 2 columns. Matrix \(B\text{:}\) 2 rows, 2 columns. The orders are the same.
    Step 2: Add corresponding entries:
    \(\begin{bmatrix} 10+7 & 12+5 \\ 8+6 & 9+11 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 17 & 17 \\ 14 & 20 \end{bmatrix}\)
    Step 3: Interpret the result.
    The resulting matrix shows the total number of items sold by both shops on each day.

    Subsection 2.1.5 Carry Out Addition and Subtraction of Matrices in Real Life Situations

    In everyday life, we often need to combine or compare data from different sourceslike adding up scores from two football matches, or finding the difference in sales between two weeks. Matrices make these calculations easy and organized!
    \(Did you know?\) Adding or subtracting matrices is just like adding or subtracting tables with the same number of rows and columns. Each entry in the result comes from adding or subtracting the corresponding entries in the original matrices.

    Matrix addition and subtraction help us combine and compare real-life data, making it easier to analyze results and spot patterns.

    Example 2.1.6.

    The following matrices show the number of apples and oranges sold by two vendors over two days:
    \(Vendor\ 1: \quad A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 7 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\) \(Vendor\ 2: \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 6 \\ 1 & 5 \end{bmatrix}\)
    Find \(A + B\) and \(A - B\text{.}\) What do the results mean?
    Solution.
    Step 1: Add the matrices:
    \(A + B = \begin{bmatrix} 5+2 & 7+6 \\ 3+1 & 4+5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 13 \\ 4 & 9 \end{bmatrix}\)
    Step 2: Subtract the matrices:
    \(A - B = \begin{bmatrix} 5-2 & 7-6 \\ 3-1 & 4-5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\)
    Step 3: Interpret the results.
    • \(A + B\) shows the total number of apples and oranges sold by both vendors each day.
    • \(A - B\) shows the difference in sales between Vendor 1 and Vendor 2 for each fruit and day. A negative number means Vendor 2 sold more than Vendor 1 for that item.

    Subsection 2.1.6 Reflecting on the Use of Matrices in Real Life Situations

    Matrices are everywhere in our daily lives! Whenever you see a table, a timetable, or a chart, you are looking at data that can be organized as a matrix. Matrices help us organize, compare, and analyze information quickly and clearly.
    \(Did you know?\) Matrices are used in science (like recording experiment results), business (sales records), sports (scoreboards), and even in technology (computer graphics and coding)!

    Activity 2.1.6.

    Investigation: Who Performed Best?
    The local sports club keeps a matrix of scores for three teams (Lions, Tigers, Eagles) over four games. The club wants to know which team performed best overall and in which game the highest score was recorded.
    Here is the matrix of scores:
    \(\begin{bmatrix} 12 & 15 & 14 & 10 \\ 10 & 18 & 13 & 12 \\ 14 & 11 & 16 & 15 \\ \end{bmatrix}\)
    (Rows: Lions, Tigers, Eagles; Columns: Game 1, Game 2, Game 3, Game 4)
    1. Work with a partner to find the total score for each team.
    2. Find the highest score in each game.
    3. Decide which team performed best overall.
    4. Identify the game with the highest single score and which team achieved it.
    5. Discuss: How does using a matrix help you answer these questions quickly?
    Key Take Away
    Matrices are powerful tools for organizing, analyzing, and solving real-life problems. They help us see patterns and make decisions based on data.

    Example 2.1.7.

    Lets solve the sports club matrix problem:
    \(\begin{bmatrix} 12 & 15 & 14 & 10 \\ 10 & 18 & 13 & 12 \\ 14 & 11 & 16 & 15 \\ \end{bmatrix}\)
    Rows: Lions, Tigers, Eagles. Columns: Game 1, Game 2, Game 3, Game 4.
    1. Total scores: Lions (12+15+14+10=51), Tigers (10+18+13+12=53), Eagles (14+11+16+15=56)
    2. Highest score in each game: Game 1 (14, Eagles), Game 2 (18, Tigers), Game 3 (16, Eagles), Game 4 (15, Eagles)
    3. Best overall: Eagles (56)
    4. Highest single score: 18 (Tigers, Game 2)
    Solution.
    Step 1: Add up each row for team totals:
    • Lions: 12+15+14+10 = 51
    • Tigers: 10+18+13+12 = 53
    • Eagles: 14+11+16+15 = 56
    Step 2: Find the highest score in each column (game):
    • Game 1: 14 (Eagles)
    • Game 2: 18 (Tigers)
    • Game 3: 16 (Eagles)
    • Game 4: 15 (Eagles)
    Step 3: The best overall team is Eagles (56 points).
    Step 4: The highest single score is 18 (Tigers, Game 2).
    Using a matrix makes it easy to compare, total, and analyze scores at a glance!
